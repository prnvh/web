## What happened

Several research groups have renewed interest in **latent prediction** — training models to forecast future states in an embedding space rather than reconstructing pixels or generating the next token in a sequence. Joint Embedding Predictive Architectures (JEPA) are the clearest articulation of this bet.

## Why it matters

If the bottleneck in world modeling is **representation quality**, not decoder fidelity, then training can focus compute on learning stable abstract states. That shifts evaluation: you care whether latents support planning and counterfactual reasoning, not whether generated frames look crisp.

## Technical context

A JEPA-style stack typically has:

1. An encoder that maps observations to embeddings.
2. A predictor that forecasts future embeddings from context.
3. A loss that pulls predicted and target embeddings together while avoiding collapse (often via masking, negative pairs, or architectural asymmetry).

The contrast with autoregressive LLMs is structural: language models optimize next-token likelihood over a discrete channel; JEPA-style models optimize **compatibility** between context and plausible futures in a continuous latent space.

## What to watch next

Whether latent predictors **compose with language models** (tool use, instruction following) or remain siloed perception modules. Also whether benchmarks emerge that measure planning-relevant latent quality — not just reconstruction proxies.
