## The claim

Autoregressive language modeling is the best general-purpose **interface** we have for knowledge work. It is not obviously the best **objective** for learning grounded, long-horizon agency.

## The context

Scaling laws and instruction tuning pushed LLMs into tool use, coding, and multimodal inputs. That success encourages a narrative: keep scaling tokens and generality follows. World-model researchers push back: **prediction in the environment’s native state space** may be necessary for reliable planning.

## The simple explanation

Next-token training asks: “What symbol comes next?” Planning asks: “If I act, what **state** comes next — and is it good?” Those are related but not identical. You can be excellent at the first while brittle at the second.

## The technical core

Autoregressive models factorize joint distributions over sequences. That is efficient for language. For control, you often want **Markovian latent dynamics** with explicit action conditioning and multi-step rollouts. Energy-based and latent-predictive objectives encode **compatibility** between context and futures — a different inductive bias.

## The strongest objection

LLMs plus retrieval, tools, and multimodal encoders may approximate world models **at the application layer** without changing the core objective. If product-level agency arrives first, laboratory world models could remain niche.

## My view

Treat next-token prediction as a **scaffold**, not a complete theory of intelligence. The interesting engineering is hybrid: language for abstraction, latent predictors for state, evals that test rollouts not single-shot answers.

## What to watch next

Benchmarks that require **closed-loop** interaction (not one-shot QA), and whether latent predictors trained at scale improve tool-using agents measurably.
